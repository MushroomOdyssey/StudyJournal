Today I would like to explore the concept of semantics so that we can appreciate the impact it has in our lives and in our HTML.

To kick things off, let's define the word itself. A quick dictionary search of the word semantic reveals the following definition:
Relating to meaning in language or logic.

That's rather vague, isn't it? Let's dig a little deeper.


When I write the word "fluffy", and you read it, there's actually a lot going on. You see a collection of symbols on a page that come together to represent a series of noises that we would make to each other if we were standing face-to-face. These noises and the symbols that represent them convey the meaning of the word as we understand it - which might change a little by region or culture.
But... it's more than that, right?
The word has a meaning outside of the symbols that we see and the sounds that we hear and even the definition that we have been taught to assign it. It conveys emotions, feelings, and maybe even memories.
All of those internal processes spring into action and you instantly associate this string of symbols with furry animals, soft tactile sensations, and plump shapes. The idea represented by each word is more than just the shape you see, or the color, or the feel, or the way it moves in the breeze, or all of these things together. I would argue that humans' understanding of a word is greater than the whole of the word's attributes.


You might be thinking,
"But this is a journal about HTML... are you telling me that a browser needs to ruminate on the deeper meaning of the <strong> element before interpreting the code?"

Well, no... of course not. And if one did, we'd have far more to worry about than understanding semantics.


But we need to understand how much more capable we are at this kind of thing than a computer is. So much of our understanding is inferred by the vast number of contextual clues we process without even really thinking about it. The browser can't think for itself (yet) and is therefor incapable of contextualizing information like a human being. We have to feed it that context manually and explicitly.

In order to understand the idea of semantics used in HTML, we have to set up a scenario where we can try to interpret information as though we were the browser - with zero context.

Ah, you finally understand what I'm asking you to do! But that was a lot of work on my end.

In turns out that we have to invest much more time, energy, and forethought into writing HTML than we do into normal conversation. This isn't because it's difficult or challenging to write HTML (or CSS), but because we aren't used to having to manually produce and connect the kind of context and information that our brains and environment normally provide for us.


So, when it comes down to it, what does this all mean for writing HTML?

It means that we have to use the resources provided to us deliberately and with a clear understanding of the impact of our choices.

HTML provides the skeleton that the rest of our webpage is built upon and we have to make sure that the foundations we are putting in place are well formed. That means we have to get to know the elements and their intended purposes, become comfortable with the hierarchy of nested elements, and know where to < > open and </ > close your elements. It also means that we will have to pay close attention to how our HTML, CSS, and eventually JavaScript interface with each other as we move forward.


These first few pages will be very simple, with a barebones layout structure and basic syntax.
